Does gaze perception influence the emergence of interpersonal coordination?

M. Catherine Macpherson, Amber J. Brown, Michael J. Richardson, & Lynden K. Miles

University of Western Australia
Macquarie University

  • Does gaze perception influence the emergence of interpersonal coordination?
Introduction

What Is Interpersonal Coordination?

Interpersonal coordination is the alignment of behaviour between people during interaction, expressed through movement, speech, and physiology.

Core Social Process

It is a fundamental feature of social exchange.

Rapport and Affiliation

When people coordinate, they often like each other more and feel a stronger connection.

Shaped by Context

Group membership and argument influence the degree of coordination.

Individual Differences

Mental health symptoms and neurodiversity can also shape how coordination unfolds.

  • To understand why this is important it's useful to first understand what coordination is and what it does in the context of social exchange.
  • Coordination is an integral part of our social world and it's something that we do all the time.
  • We align our body movements, speech patterns, and even our physiology with our interaction partners.
  • When individuals coordinate there are prosocial outcomes - they often report enhanced rapport and affiliation.
  • But coordination is also shaped by context - factors such as group membership or argumentative versus affiliative contexts can influence the degree to which coordination emerges.
  • And finally, it's also influenced by individual differences such as symptoms of social anxiety.

Coupling influences coordination

Visual tracking strengthens coordination in minimally social contexts (Richardson et al., 2007; Schmidt et al., 2007).

What about in social settings?

Individuals show subtle differences in gaze and attention as a function of context.

Research Question 1

Do patterns of social attention (via gaze and focus) govern coordination?

  • A key question underlying much of this work is: how is coordination shaped by contextual and individual factors?
  • To answer this, we can get insight into theoretical models of rhythmic coordination such as the HKB model.
  • This suggests that coordination is shaped by two parameters - individuals should be moving at similar frequencies and they need to be coupled in some way - often via vision.
  • The coupling parameter has been validated in interpersonal coordination through a series of heavy handed manipulations in which individuals were asked to visually track the object or person they were coordinating with, across simple rhythmic tasks such as pendulum swinging or rocking in a chair.
  • These found that people coordinate more with increased visual tracking.
  • But these experiments were done in highly constrained and minimally social contexts - what about in social settings?
  • Do subtle differences in coupling, by shifting gaze or attention, shape coordination in more real world-style interactions?
  • This became our first research question.
Introduction

The Dual Function of Gaze

  • Gathering information: coupling or decoupling from an interaction partner.
  • A social signal:
    • communicating behaviours such as affiliative intent...
    • ...or withdrawal and disengagement.

Research Question 2

Does partner gaze influence the emergence of coordination?

  • We planned to capture this in part by using eye tracking while participants engaged in a coordination task, that we'll get to later.
  • But of course gaze is not just designed to gather information and aid coupling between individuals.
  • It's also used as a social signal - one that can communicate behaviours such as affiliative intent or withdrawal.
  • And so we didn't just want to look at the gaze behaviours of our participants in terms of tracking, but also how they responded to the gaze behaviours of an interaction partner.
  • So this became our second research question.
  • Across a series of studies, we examined this.
Studies

Study 1 & 2: Methods

Procedure
  • Participants: 133 (study 1) and 150 (study 2) undergraduate students.
  • Task: performed a coordination task with 'another participant' - actually a pre-programmed avatar.
  • Manipulations: the avatar displayed either direct or averted gaze (study 1 & 2) and participant focused on self or other (study 2).

  • Now I'm going to present a couple of studies aimed at answering these questions.
  • Here we asked participants to perform a simple coordination task, arm curls, in virtual reality with an avatar who they were told was a real participant they'd meet later.
  • But in reality, it was a pre-programmed avatar.
  • And importantly we added a gaze manipulation so that participants were randomly assigned to interact with an avatar who displayed either direct or averted gaze for the duration of the interaction.
  • VR afforded both this and the capturing of participant gaze behaviours during the interaction.
  • In the second study, we also added a second manipulation - asking participants to focus on either themselves and monitor their own actions, or to focus on their interaction partner for the duration of the interaction.
  • This was done in the knowledge that gaze behaviours don't necessarily index attention entirely.
  • We captured movement from both the arms, in both studies, and, for study 2, the head of the participant and avatar.
  • So that we could capture interpersonal coordination of both the head and arm movements.
Studies

Study 1 & 2: Results

Participant Gaze

  • Arm coordination: higher with more time looking at the avatar (study 1).
  • Head coordination: higher with more time looking at the avatar in the other-focused condition (study 2).

Partner Gaze

  • Arm coordination: higher in direct than averted (study 1 & 2).
  • Head coordination: higher in averted when in the other-focused condition (study 2).

Results

  • What did we find?
  • For the plots, higher numbers equal greater coordination.
  • In terms of answering RQ1, do subtle changes in participant gaze shape coordination, we found that yes they generally do.
  • In study 1, arm coordination was higher with more time spent looking at the avatar.
  • This was the same in study 2 for head coordination; however, in the self-focus condition this relationship actually reversed.
  • And so this relationship is not as simple as gaze direction - an individual's focus of attention needs to be taken into account.
  • For RQ2, does partner gaze influence coordination - again yes, although the direction of the effect was shaped by modality.
  • For the instructed behaviour, arm curls, coordination was higher in the direct gaze condition.
  • For the head movements, in the other-focused condition, the opposite occurred - coordination was actually higher in the averted gaze condition.
Studies

Study 1 & 2: Discussion

Findings

  • Gaze perception influences coordination.
  • Arm coordination increased when the avatar looked directly at the participant.

Why?

  • Gaze specified affiliative intent.
  • Direct gaze invites engagement whereas averted gaze may encourage disinterest.

However...

  • Participant focus of attention matters.
  • Avatar averted gaze increased head coordination in the other-focused condition.

Why?

  • Social exclusion often encourages withdrawal, but can also lead to attempts to (re)connect.
  • Mixed goals?
  • What might be going on here?
  • In terms of the main finding that arm coordination increased when the avatar looks directly at the participant...
  • It is likely that gaze specified affiliative intent.
  • Direct gaze invites engagement whereas averted gaze may encourage disinterest.
  • For head movements however we saw the opposite pattern - when participants focused on the avatar, averted gaze led to higher levels of head coordination.
  • This requires a follow up study to disentangle, but social exclusion, for example being ignored, often encourages withdrawal, as seen in the arm coordination.
  • It can also lead people to ingratiate in an attempt to reconnect.
  • So there may be mixed goals that are revealed in the dynamics of the interaction.
  • Large movements showing withdrawal, as in the arms, alongside approach in the more subtle head movements for increased social connection.
  • Speculative.

Study 3: Method

Procedure
  • Participants: 134 undergraduate students.
  • Task: performed a coordination task with 'another participant' - actually a pre-programmed avatar.
  • Manipulation: avatar performed either congruent or incongruent gaze.
Manipulations
Congruent

Measurement
  • Motion capture: from the HTC Vive controllers.
  • Gaze tracking: eye tracking from the HTC Vive headset.
  • Coordination: cross-correlation of arm movements between the avatar and participant.
  • In study 3 we took a slightly different approach and examined how the reliability of gaze cues influence coordination.
  • This was in an effort to see whether the influence of partner gaze would generalise to more functional tasks, such as those with a performance related outcome.
  • So we designed a study, again in VR, in which individuals were asked to slide a controller towards the same target as a partner.
  • Preceding each trial, the partner displayed a congruent or incongruent cue.
  • So they looked either towards or away from the target on the table.
  • As in the last study, we captured movement coordination, this time as a cross-correlation.
Studies

Study 3: Results

Gaze Congruency

  • Coordination: higher following congruent gaze cues.
  • Pattern: classic congruency effects replicated using interpersonal coordination.

Social Anxiety

  • Moderation: congruency effects were amplified at higher social anxiety.
  • Implication: gaze perception may shape how anxiety influences coordination.

Results

  • Again, coordination is plotted up the side of the graph and split by condition.
  • And higher numbers indicate more stable coordination.
  • We replicated classic congruency effects, but using coordination.
  • Individuals were more coordinated with a partner when the cue preceding the movement was congruent than incongruent.
  • Interestingly, this effect was amplified in individuals higher in social anxiety - suggesting that gaze perception plays a role in how social anxiety shapes coordination.
Summary

Conclusion

Gaze shapes the emergence of interpersonal coordination across distinct social contexts.

Gaze Patterns

Coordination changed across direct versus averted gaze, and congruent versus incongruent cues.

Individual Differences

These effects were shaped by focus of attention and social anxiety.

Contextual Influence

Gaze patterns may shape how coordination emerges across various contexts (such as argument).

  • Taking this all together, gaze shapes the emergence of interpersonal coordination across distinct social contexts.
  • First, participant and partner gaze patterns influenced coordination.
  • This was evident across direct versus averted gaze, and congruent versus incongruent cues.
  • Second, these effects were shaped by individual differences.
  • In particular, focus of attention and social anxiety both changed the way gaze related to coordination.
  • Finally, these findings may help us understand other contextual influences on coordination.
  • For example, gaze patterns during argument may facilitate or diminish alignment between interaction partners.
