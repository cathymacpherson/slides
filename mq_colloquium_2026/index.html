Interpersonal Coordination

Capturing interpersonal behaviour in social and clinical settings

Framing

What Is Interpersonal Coordination?

Interpersonal coordination is the alignment of behaviour between people during interaction, expressed through movement, speech, and physiology.

Core Social Process

It is a fundamental feature of social exchange.

Rapport and Affiliation

When people coordinate, they often like each other more and feel a stronger connection.

Prosocial Outcomes

Coordination is associated with greater willingness to help and cooperate.

Individual Differences

Mental health symptoms and neurodiversity can also shape how coordination unfolds.

  • First, what do I mean by interpersonal coordination?
  • I mean the alignment of behaviour between individuals, and that can be expressed through movement, speech, or physiology.
  • We study it because it is a core part of social exchange and is linked with positive outcomes like rapport, affiliation, and helping.
  • But it is also shaped by context, social boundaries, and individual differences.
  • This is where my own work sits: asking how mental health symptoms and neurodiversity shape coordination.
Prior work

Mental Health and Coordination

Neurodiversity and certain mental health disorders undermine interpersonal coordination.

Social Anxiety

Lower coordination stability when individuals diagnosed with social anxiety disorder led coordination (Varlet et al., 2014).

Schizophrenia

Lower coordination stability in individuals with schizophrenia (Varlet et al., 2012).

Autism

Autistic children showed less stable and more deterministic patterns of coordination (Romero et al., 2016).

  • Prior work suggests that neurodiversity and certain mental health disorders can undermine interpersonal coordination.
  • Examples include social anxiety, schizophrenia, and autism.
  • This background motivates the questions I take up in my own work.
Foundational work

Starting Simple: Rhythmic Coordination

  • Approach: Began with simple rhythmic tasks such as pendulum swinging.
  • Findings: Higher levels of traits associated with social anxiety, generalised anxiety, and autism were linked with less stable coordination.
  • Outcome: It established a basic relationship between subclinical symptom profiles and interpersonal coordination.
  • To capture the influence of these symptoms on social behaviour, I started with simple rhythmic tasks such as pendulum swinging.
  • Across two studies, we found that higher levels of traits associated with disorders such as social anxiety and generalised anxiety were associated with less stable coordination.
  • This gave us a clean starting point for establishing the relationship.
Virtual reality studies

Does Partner Behaviour Matter?

Using VR to manipulate partner gaze to examine whether this shapes the relationship between anxiety and coordination.

Key result

Higher social anxiety predicted more stable coordination in the averted-gaze condition — reduced social scrutiny facilitated coordination.

Key result

Higher social anxiety predicted an even larger drop in coordination with incongruent cues — the disruption was amplified.

  • Having established this pattern in rhythmic tasks and replicated clinical findings sub-clinically, we began to ask how your interaction partner's behaviour might influence coordination.
  • To answer this, we needed to control partner behaviour — so we moved into virtual reality.
  • In Study 1, participants did arm curls with an avatar who either maintained direct gaze or looked largely away and shifted gaze frequently.
  • People generally coordinated more with the direct-gaze avatar, but those higher in social anxiety showed the opposite: they coordinated more when the avatar looked away.
  • We interpreted this as reduced social scrutiny giving space for coordination to emerge.
  • In Study 2, participants performed an object movement task with an avatar, and each movement was preceded by a congruent or incongruent head movement from the avatar.
  • People generally coordinated better with congruent cues, but those higher in social anxiety showed an even greater drop in the incongruent condition.
  • Together, these results show that the relationship between social anxiety and coordination is not fixed — it is responsive to the social environment.

From Simple Tasks To Real Interaction

Coordination in everyday life is more complex, multimodal, and socially embedded.

  • You'll notice that the previous studies used simple movement tasks like pendulum swinging and arm curls.
  • But in day-to-day life, we coordinate in a much more complex and multimodal way.
Methodological history

Why We Started Simple

  • Physical sensors were the gold standard — but they must be attached to the body, limiting capture to individual body parts.
  • Full-body capture required more: multi-sensor suits and infrared camera systems that were expensive, time-consuming to set up, and intrusive.
  • The cost of precision: the more accurate the capture, the less natural the experiment.
  • It turns out that moving to real interaction is not straightforward.
  • Physical sensors have been the gold standard, but they require attachment to the body and are best suited to capturing individual body parts only.
  • More complex setups — multi-sensor suits, infrared camera systems — have historically been expensive, time-consuming, and take a certain element of realism from the experiment.
Computer vision revolution

A New Landscape

  • Standard webcams can now capture socially meaningful movement in real time.
  • Pose estimation tools like MediaPipe and OpenPose extract detailed positional data across hundreds of keypoints.
  • The whole body, in context — face, head, torso, and limbs during natural interaction.
  • Thankfully, recent advances in computer vision have completely changed the landscape of motion capture.
  • A standard webcam can now be used to capture movement.
  • Pose estimation tools like MediaPipe and OpenPose can extract detailed positional data across hundreds of keypoints — face, head, torso, and limbs — during natural interaction.
Post-processing

Capturing Data Is Only The Start

  • Rich but complex: pose estimation produces hundreds of samples of positional data per second across multiple people.
  • Coordination requires comparability: we need to remove bias from camera angles and handle the occlusion of keypoints.
  • No established guidelines existed for post-processing pose data in dyadic coordination research.
MiND lab contribution

Led by a PhD student in the lab, we developed best-practice guidelines for post-processing pose estimation data in coordination research.

  • These advances seemed like a great option for moving the work towards capturing real-time interaction.
  • But as we began to collect and analyse this data, we realised things were more complicated than we first thought.
  • These algorithms capture hundreds of samples of positional data, but quantifying coordination relies on being able to compare people without bias from camera angles or keypoint occlusion.
  • Given how new these tools were, there weren't any best practice guidelines for post-processing pose data.
  • And so, led by one of the wonderful PhD students in the MiND lab, we wrote some.

Mosaic study

Coordination In Noisy Environments

  • Collaborated with linguists Kelly Miles and Jörg Buchholz to study conversation under increasing background noise.
  • Pairs wore acoustically open headphones and conversed across four noise conditions: office, café, food court, and party.
  • Used pose estimation, velocity vectors, and recurrence analysis to quantify interpersonal coordination.
Key result

Facial and upper body coordination both increased as background noise rose — suggesting coordination may facilitate communication under difficult conditions.

  • This study was run by wonderful colleagues in linguistics — Kelly Miles and Jörg Buchholz — and aimed to examine the influence of background noise on conversation and communication breakdowns.
  • Participants came to the lab in pairs and conversed while wearing acoustically open headphones, exposed to increasing background noise from an office, café, food court, and party.
  • We used pose estimation to extract positional data, converted it into velocity vectors, and ran recurrence analysis to determine the degree to which coordination emerged.
  • We found that individuals exhibited a higher degree of coordination across both facial and upper body movements as background noise rose.
  • This suggests that coordination may serve to facilitate communication under difficult environmental constraints.
Macquarie Lighthouse Fellowship

Coordination In Psychotherapy

  • A long-standing goal: use pose estimation to capture coordination in therapy — with the aim of predicting and improving outcomes.
  • Why this works: coordination indexes interaction quality, and therapeutic alliance is one of the strongest predictors of therapy outcomes.
  • Existing work is limited: single session recordings that capture gross body movement through pixel changes.
  • Our goal: capture how coordination dynamics fluctuate across the arc of a full course of therapy.
Related preprint

Carter Sale (PhD student, MiND lab) models patterns of affect dynamics across therapy sessions. We are currently linking these to pose-based coordination data.

  • Interpersonal coordination indexes interaction quality, and the therapeutic alliance is a key driver in psychotherapy outcomes.
