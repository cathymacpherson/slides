Introduction

What Is Interpersonal Coordination?

Interpersonal coordination is the alignment of behaviour between people during interaction, expressed through movement, speech, and physiology.

Core Social Process

It is a fundamental feature of social exchange.

Rapport and Affiliation

When people coordinate, they often like each other more and feel a stronger connection.

Prosocial Outcomes

Coordination is associated with greater willingness to help and cooperate.

Individual Differences

Mental health symptoms and neurodiversity can also shape how coordination unfolds.

  • Over the last few years, I have been broadly interested in studying interpersonal coordination.
  • This is the alignment of behaviours such as movement, speech, or physiology between people during interaction.
  • Why study coordination? It’s a fundamental feature of social exchange — something we do all the time.
  • It also has social benefits: when people coordinate, they often report higher levels of rapport and affiliation.
  • Coordination is associated with prosocial behaviours, such as a greater willingness to help others.
  • Individual differences, including those associated with mental health disorders or neurodiversity, can shape the degree to which coordination emerges.
  • This has been the focus of much of my work.
Prior work

Mental Health and Coordination

  • Neurodiversity and certain mental health disorders undermine interpersonal coordination.
  • Do subclinical traits account for some of the variation that we see in coordination generally?

Social Anxiety

Lower coordination stability when individuals diagnosed with social anxiety disorder led coordination (Varlet et al., 2014).

Schizophrenia

Lower coordination stability in individuals with schizophrenia (Varlet et al., 2012).

Autism

Autistic children showed less stable and more deterministic patterns of coordination (Romero et al., 2016).

  • When I started my PhD, there were a small number of studies looking at the influence of mental health and neurodiversity on coordination.
  • The results were pretty consistent: those with diagnoses such as social anxiety, schizophrenia, and autism exhibited less coordination than individuals without a diagnosis.
  • But the number of studies was limited and constrained to small samples of clinical populations.
  • We decided to measure relevant traits in subclinical populations.
  • The goal was to see whether individual differences could explain some of the variation that we see in coordination more generally.
Foundational work

Starting Simple: Rhythmic Coordination

  • Approach: Began with simple rhythmic tasks such as pendulum swinging.
  • Findings: Higher levels of traits associated with anxiety and autism were linked with less stable coordination.
  • Outcome: It established a basic relationship between subclinical symptom profiles and interpersonal coordination.
Related paper

Macpherson, M. C., Marie, D., Schön, S., & Miles, L. K. (2020). Evaluating the interplay between subclinical levels of mental health symptoms and coordination dynamics. British Journal of Psychology.

  • We started simple, adopting a well validated task from the previous literature.
  • Participants came into the lab and coordinated the swinging of handheld pendulums.
  • We also asked them to fill out questionnaires designed to capture social anxiety, generalised anxiety, depression symptoms, and autistic traits.
  • Across two studies, higher levels of anxiety and autistic traits were associated with less stable patterns of interpersonal coordination.
  • This relationship was constrained to certain symptom profiles: symptoms of depression showed no relationship.
Virtual reality studies

Does Partner Behaviour Matter?

Using VR to manipulate partner gaze to examine whether this shapes the relationship between anxiety and coordination.

Study 1 — Gaze direction during arm curls

Averted Gaze
Key result

Higher social anxiety predicted more stable coordination in the averted-gaze condition — reduced social scrutiny facilitated coordination.

Study 2 — Congruent vs. incongruent head movement
Congruent Gaze
Incongruent Gaze
Key result

Higher social anxiety predicted an even larger drop in coordination with incongruent cues — the disruption was amplified.

  • Having established this pattern, and replicated clinical findings in subclinical samples, we began to wonder how the behaviour of an interaction partner might influence this relationship.
  • To answer this, we needed a way to control or manipulate partner behaviour, so we moved into virtual reality.
  • Across a series of studies, we gave participants simple movement tasks, such as arm curls.
  • We looked at the degree to which a partner’s gaze behaviour influenced the emergence of coordination.
  • Ran a series of studies using this design - I'm going to talk about a couple
  • In the first study, participants performed arm curls with an avatar who they were told was another participant in a different room
  • The avatar either looked directly at them for the trial or looked largely away and shifted gaze frequently.
  • People generally coordinated more with the avatar who looked directly at them.
  • Individuals higher in social anxiety showed the opposite effect: they coordinated more in the averted gaze condition.
  • It seemed that the reduction in social scrutiny afforded by averted partner gaze gave coordination the opportunity to emerge.
  • In another study, we used a more goal-directed method: participants slid a controller towards a blue target at the same time as their partner.
  • Each movement was preceded by the avatar making either a congruent or incongruent head movement, towards or away from the target.
  • People in general were more coordinated with the avatar when it displayed a congruent head movement.
  • Individuals higher in social anxiety showed an even greater difference, with even lower coordination in the incongruent condition than their peers.
  • Together, these results suggest that the relationship between coordination and mental health, particularly social anxiety, is not fixed.
  • It is responsive to the social environment.

From Simple Tasks To Real Interaction

Coordination in everyday life is more complex, multimodal, and socially embedded.

  • The sticking point in all of this work is the tasks used.
  • Classic coordination research, including my own, has focused on simple rhythmic tasks.
  • But the real world is much more complex than that.
  • In conversation, we align our behaviours in nonlinear ways across multiple modalities, such as movement and language.
  • I wanted to scale up and capture coordination as it unfolds in everyday life.
Methodological history

From Sensors To Pose Estimation

Physical Sensors

  • Attach directly to the body.
  • Capture precise movement from selected body parts.
  • Often expensive and time-consuming to set up.

Pose Estimation

  • Works from ordinary video or webcam footage.
  • Extracts keypoints across the whole body.
  • Preserves natural interaction contexts.
  • It turns out that this isn’t necessarily straightforward.
  • There are many reasons why we have historically focused on simple rhythmic movements.
  • Physical sensors have been the gold standard, but these rely on being attached to the body.
  • They are often best suited to capturing individual body parts only.
  • More complex setups, such as multi-sensor suits or infrared camera systems, have historically been expensive and time-consuming to set up.
  • They can also take a certain element of realism from the experiment when participants are dressed up like this.
  • Thankfully, recent advances in computer vision have completely changed the landscape of motion capture.
  • Now, a standard webcam can be used to capture movement.
  • Pose estimation tools such as MediaPipe and OpenPose can extract detailed positional data across hundreds of keypoints.
New Analysis Pipeline

Pose Estimation Challenges

  • Challenge: Hundreds of samples, biased by camera angle/position.
  • Best practice guidelines: Didn't really exist.
  • Analysis framework: Step-by-step from acquisition and feature selection through to linear and recurrence analysis.
Related preprint

Sale, C., Macpherson, M. C., Patil, G., Miles, K., Kallen, R., Wallot, S., & Richardson, M. J. (2026). Nonlinear methods for analyzing pose in behavioral research.

  • These advances seemed like a great option for moving my work towards capturing real-time interaction.
  • But as we began to collect and analyse this data, we realised that things were more complicated than we first thought.
  • These algorithms capture hundreds of samples worth of positional data.
  • Quantifying coordination relies on being able to compare people and their movements without bias associated with camera angles or the occlusion of keypoints.
  • Given how new these tools were, there weren’t really any best practice guidelines for effectively post-processing pose data.
  • Over the past couple of years, led by one of the wonderful PhD students in the MiND Lab, we wrote some.
Case study

Coordination In Noisy Environments

  • Question: Does interpersonal coordination fluctuate as a function of background noise?
  • Approach: pairs of people converse while listening to various background noises.
  • Analysis: pose estimation to extract movement data from cameras, then cross-recurrence analysis.
  • Key finding: individuals exhibited a higher degree of facial and upper body coordination as background noise rose.
Key implication

Coordination may serve to facilitate communication under difficult environmental constraints.

  • In developing these guidelines, we used data from a number of studies.
  • Today I want to talk about one in particular that I’ve been involved in over the past couple of years.
  • This study was run by wonderful colleagues in linguistics, including Kelly Miles and Jorg Buchholz.
  • It aimed to examine the influence of background noise on conversation, and in particular on communication breakdowns.
  • People came to the lab in pairs and had conversations while wearing acoustically open headphones.
  • These headphones allow people to converse normally while being exposed to increasing background noise.
  • The study used noises from an office environment, café, food court, and party.
  • Participants were filmed throughout the trials.
  • Given that coordination is such a fundamental part of interaction, I was interested in whether interpersonal coordination fluctuates as a function of background noise.
  • We used pose estimation to extract positional data from the cameras.
  • We converted this data into velocity vectors so that we could look at the dynamics of the interaction.
  • We then ran recurrence analysis to determine the degree to which coordination between interactants emerged.
  • Individuals exhibited a higher degree of coordination across both facial and upper body movements as background noise rose.
  • This suggests that coordination may serve to facilitate communication under difficult environmental constraints.
Macquarie University Lighthouse Fellowship

Coordination In Psychotherapy

  • Goal: Use pose estimation to capture coordination in therapy — to predict and improve outcomes.
  • Idea: Coordination indexes interaction quality, and therapeutic alliance is a strong predictor of therapy outcomes.
  • Existing work: Single session recordings that capture gross body movement.
  • Our goal: Capture how coordination dynamics fluctuate within and between therapy sessions.
Related preprint

Sale, C., Macpherson, M., Zheng, Y., Richardson, M., & Peluso, P. (2026). State-dependent dynamics of therapist-client affect in psychotherapy.

  • I want to finish up by turning back to mental health and talking about some of the more applied benefits of these techniques.
  • I’ll reference a project that I’m currently working on and will be working on for the next couple of years.
  • When I began to capture coordination in real-world interactions, I had a secret, or maybe not so secret, secondary goal.
  • That goal was to use these techniques to capture coordination in psychotherapy settings, with the goal of predicting and improving therapy outcomes.
  • Why do this? Interpersonal coordination indexes interaction quality, and the therapeutic alliance is a key driver in psychotherapy outcomes.
  • This idea sits on a strong foundation of work that has captured movement coordination in therapy.
  • But existing work is typically limited to single-session recordings that capture gross body movement through pixel changes.
  • Our goal is to capture fluctuations in the dynamics that emerge between therapist and client across therapy sessions.
  • This work is in its early stages, so I don’t have much in the way of results to present here.
  • However, I wanted to highlight a related preprint we recently released, led by a PhD student in the lab, Carter Sale.
  • That preprint models patterns of affect dynamics across therapy sessions.
  • I’m currently analysing pose data from these sessions to determine whether we can use movement to predict affective states that occur during therapy.
